Overview

About the Book

From the Book

The Big Idea

"AI snake oil" is AI that doesn't work as advertised — and probably can't. The book argues that the blanket term "AI" obscures the reality that very different technologies are lumped together. ChatGPT has almost nothing in common with the software banks use to evaluate loan applicants, but both get called "AI."

The authors draw a crucial distinction between predictive AI (systems making decisions about people — loans, parole, hiring, healthcare) and generative AI (systems like ChatGPT producing text, images, and code). The risks — and the potential — are very different.

⚠️ Predictive AI — Where Snake Oil Thrives

Used in hiring, criminal justice, healthcare, insurance. The book argues this technology hasn't meaningfully improved in 50 years for predicting individual behavior.

✅ AI That Actually Works

Spell-check, autocomplete, spam filters, speech recognition, robot vacuums. Once these worked well, we stopped calling them "AI." Generative AI is genuinely promising when used wisely.

About the Authors

Arvind Narayanan & Sayash Kapoor

Arvind Narayanan is a professor of computer science at Princeton and director of its Center for Information Technology Policy. His research was among the first to show how machine learning reflects cultural stereotypes. He received the Presidential Early Career Award for Scientists and Engineers and previously co-authored Bitcoin and Cryptocurrency Technologies (Princeton, 2016).

Sayash Kapoor is a PhD candidate at Princeton. Before academia, he worked as a software engineer at Facebook building AI for content moderation — he's seen these issues from the inside.

Together they were named to TIME's inaugural 100 Most Influential People in AI. They co-author the newsletter AI as Normal Technology (formerly AI Snake Oil), read by ~50,000 researchers, policymakers, and journalists.

Live Discussion Recap

From the Live Session

Guest Author Q&A with Arvind Narayanan

Arvind joined for the opening 20 minutes before another commitment. Below are the key highlights from his remarks and the group conversation that followed.

🔮 His New Project: "AI as Normal Technology"

The book focused on present-day hype. His new project examines the next 20 years. He compares AI's long-run impact to general-purpose technologies like electricity, the internet, and the Industrial Revolution — transformative, but gradual rather than overnight.

A grounded, long-horizon view can reduce panic while still preparing early for real challenges.

😱 "People Are Afraid of the Wrong Things"

Arvind cited Bruce Schneier's principle: If it's in the news, don't worry about it. Rare, sensational risks get attention; common, large-scale harms get missed.

Underestimated threat: Non-consensual deepfake nude images — a 2019 report found ~100,000 already created and traded, primarily targeting women and girls, occurring globally. Policymakers only recently began responding.

Overestimated threat: AI-driven election interference. Analysis of 2024 elections across ~60 countries showed AI election interference did not emerge as a major factor.

🏥 Healthcare AI: "These Models Are Not Getting Better"

Arvind was blunt: predictive AI in healthcare has not meaningfully improved.

UnitedHealthcare case: 90% of AI decisions found incorrect, yet employees were required to follow them or face firing.

Key insight: Models can't meaningfully improve for individual outcomes because of inherent uncertainty. They also pick up sensitive patterns (race, gender) even when those fields aren't explicitly included, through proxies like residential location.

His prescription: Solutions are primarily non-technical — decide when not to use such tools, and when used, add procedural protections and genuine human oversight.

🎓 AI in Education: The Calculator Analogy

The comparison to calculators can sound flippant, but Arvind stands by it: education will be "okay" long-term, though the transition is "tumultuous" and "deeply confusing."

Each educator must determine the line between essential skills (critical thinking, judgment, design tradeoffs) and incidental skills (syntax, formatting) that AI can handle.

Group members raised oral defenses as assessment and discussed feasibility in large classes.

👷 Invisible Labor & a New Worker Movement

Data annotation workers in Kenya are paid under $4/hour to label toxic content that makes chatbots safe. Arvind estimated a lower bound of millions of such workers; companies are secretive about the true scale.

He sees the need for a new, international worker consciousness and movement, comparable to what followed the Industrial Revolution.

📋 Additional Examples from the Discussion

Netherlands welfare-fraud algorithm (2013–2019): Wrongly accused ~30,000 parents, often using nationality. Some faced debts over €100,000, leading to the 2021 resignation of the prime minister and cabinet.

Michigan unemployment algorithm (2013–2015): Wrongly collected $21 million.

Australia's "robo-debt" scandal: 721 million AUD wrongly collected.

Toronto beach predictions (2022): Vendor promised 90% accuracy, but on 64% of unsafe days, beaches remained open.

Key Examples

From the Shortform Summary

How Predictive AI Goes Wrong

Healthcare Optum's "Impact Pro" — Used healthcare spending as a proxy for needs. Because Black patients historically had less access, the system systematically deprioritized them.
Healthcare The Asthma/Pneumonia Paradox — Algorithm deemed asthma patients lower risk because they had better outcomes in training data (due to aggressive treatment). Replacing the system would send them home with potentially fatal results.
Healthcare Epic's Sepsis System — Hospitals adopted it despite unverified efficacy, incentivized by monetary rewards. Multiple analyses found outcomes weren't up to par.
Criminal Justice COMPAS Recidivism Tool — Biased against Black defendants and no more accurate than simple rules based on age and prior convictions.
Hiring Retorio Video Interviews — Changes in appearance, backdrop, or resume arrangement changed AI assessments. Candidates gamed it with invisible text and jargon.
Policing ShotSpotter — Hasn't reduced gun violence despite claims. Led to wrongful arrests. Ignores systemic causes of violence.
Research Fragile Families Challenge — With 10,000+ data points per child, complex AI performed no better than a simple model with a handful of basic variables.
From the Shortform Summary

Content Moderation Failures

Facebook in Myanmar: One Burmese-speaking moderator for an entire country. Despite years of warnings, hate speech fueled violence that killed over 10,000 and drove 700,000+ from their homes.

Absurd automated decisions: X suspended an anti-Nazi Captain America cartoon. YouTube removed Cornell's video library over one art lecture. YouTube flagged a chess video for "White is better."

Medical photo misidentification: A father lost Google access after AI flagged his child's medical photos as exploitation.

From the Book

Where AI Is Actually Helpful

The book is emphatic that it is not anti-AI. Technologies that work well: spell-check, autocomplete, spam filters, speech recognition, handwriting recognition, robot vacuums, and airplane autopilot. Once AI works reliably, we stop calling it "AI."

Generative AI is acknowledged as a genuinely powerful creative tool. Self-driving cars could eventually prevent roughly 1 million fatalities worldwide annually. AI in education can be a valuable learning tool — comparable to the calculator — with the right oversight.
